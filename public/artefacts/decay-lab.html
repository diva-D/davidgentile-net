The skill decay lab

Every app that calls a skill "mastered" is lying to you. Real skills have two meters: one that ratchets up when you prove you can, and one that quietly drifts down when you don't practice. Play with this to feel the difference.

Below is a small practice green. Eight drills. Each has a freshness meter — how sharp you are on that skill, right now. Every day, all of them drift down a little. Click a drill to practice it (costs one day, restores freshness to 100%). Press Skip day to pass a day without practicing. Try to keep everything sharp for a month.

Avg freshness 100%
You're at full sharpness. Now skip a day. Skip seven. Watch what happens.

Notice the tension: you have exactly one practice per day, and more skills than you can maintain. The question isn't can I master this? — it's which skills do I let drift this week?

This is the shape every skill app is wrong about. Mastery is not a checkbox. A skill you proved you could do last April is not a skill you have today. Freshness decays quietly, and no ✓ in an app is going to hold back time.

A practice product that treats mastery and freshness as separate meters — and decides your daily session from the freshness map, not the mastery map — is a fundamentally different product than the ones that ship today. They have the wrong model of what a skill is.

The uncomfortable part: as you unlock more skills, you can't maintain them all. The system forces a choice about what to let go. That choice is the craft. It's also what no current app helps you make.

What this is. A working artefact — a tiny explorable in the tradition of Bret Victor, Nicky Case, Bartosz Ciechanowski. You shared the idea of skill decay in 400 words; the demo lets you feel it in 90 seconds. The argument isn't in the copy — it's in what happens to the bars when you try to keep them all green.
