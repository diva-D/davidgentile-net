The friction tax

Two people. Same twelve items landed on them this morning. Same brain, same AI, same workday ahead. The only difference: one runs on loose notes, the other runs on typed objects with states, deadlines, and next actions. Watch the tax compound.

Same work. Same person. Same AI. One has ontology, one doesn't. The question on the page is just: what needs my attention right now? Try to answer it from the left panel. Then try from the right.

The friction tax isn't the work. It's the re-reading. The remembering what state this thing was in. The digging through Slack to find what Priya said on Tuesday. Every item without type, state, and a next action charges rent on your attention, every time you look at the pile.

GTD was ontology engineering in disguise. Inbox → Next Action → Waiting-On → Someday/Maybe is a tiny schema — type, status, owner — enforced by hand with paper and folders. It worked because the structure did the thinking. It was brutal to maintain because humans are slow, boring parsers.

AI removes the tax on producing structure. It does not remove the need for structure. The leverage isn't in better chat — it's in letting the model turn your unstructured exhaust into typed objects the moment it lands, so every downstream question becomes a filter instead of a re-read.

Simulate a day passing. Watch the left panel drift — items go stale, two quietly slip past their deadline, nothing tells you. The right panel reshuffles: Waiting-on items that timed out move to Needs action now. The schema notices. The pile doesn't.

This is the whole argument for the structure layer in one screen. Every org running on unstructured exhaust is paying this tax, every hour, and has been paying it forever. Until now it was just the cost of work. Now it's a choice.
